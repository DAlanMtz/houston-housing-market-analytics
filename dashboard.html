Balanced, and trending cooler after a sharp 2020-2022 surge.

About this project

A public, descriptive business analytics project studying Houston-area housing conditions from public housing and economic data. Built as four additive datasets, each with its own notebook and case study: core metro-level trends from Redfin, macro and affordability context (oil, employment, rent, an independent price index, income, inflation), county-level submarket variation with one clearly-bounded baseline forecast, and city-level submarket variation one grain finer still. No proprietary MLS data is used or redistributed anywhere in this project.

Core market trends (Redfin, 2012-2026)

Sales activity, pricing, and inventory moved through recognizable multi-year cycles: a mid-2010s warm stretch, a long balanced period, a sharp pandemic-era surge, and a cooling stretch running into 2026. Months of inventory and days on market move together closely (correlation around 0.83) across the full period.

Macro and affordability context

Three independently computed price signals (Redfin, Zillow ZHVI, FHFA HPI) move together closely, and Houston's energy-linked economy tracks the local market condition score more visibly than the national mortgage rate does. About 53% of the nominal 2012-2026 price increase is inflation rather than real appreciation, and real prices actually peaked in 2022.

Baseline forecast (R, ETS) · a bounded reference point, not a prediction

This project is descriptive everywhere else. This is the one deliberate exception: a naive, mechanical six-month extrapolation of each series' own history (R's forecast::ets, automatic model selection), with no access to the mortgage rate, oil price, employment, or any other variable studied elsewhere in this project.

City submarkets: how far down does the variation go?

The city of Houston alone is about 37% of total metro-city sales volume, more than 3x the next-largest city (Katy). But of the 165 cities Redfin tracks within the Houston metro, only 16 average at least 50 sales a month; the other 149 (90%) are flagged thin and too noisy to read month to month. Even among the 16 reliable cities, price appreciation and inventory pressure still vary meaningfully, the same pattern found at the county level, one grain finer.

Limitations, caveats & scope

Scope · No causal claims

Every relationship shown across all four waves (oil vs. market condition, employment vs. sales, rate and oil lags) is descriptive or correlational. The one Granger causality test measures predictive information content in a time-series sense, not a real-world causal mechanism.

Geography · Sources don't share one boundary

Redfin, Zillow, and FHFA use comparable but not boundary-identical Houston metro definitions. City of Houston permits cover only the city jurisdiction. Harris County income is narrower than the full metro. The mortgage rate and oil price are national. See data/source_notes.md for the full table.

County data · Four counties are thin

Waller, Chambers, Liberty, and Austin counties average under 80 sales a month. Their statistics are noisy and can swing to extreme values from ordinary small-sample variation, not real market moves.

City data · 149 of 165 cities are thin

Cities averaging under 50 sales a month are flagged thin_market and excluded from every city chart, though they remain in the underlying CSV. Several are thin enough that Redfin itself does not compute months-of-inventory or year-over-year figures for them.

Composite metrics · Not official indices

affordability_pressure_index and market_condition_score are simple, transparent, self-documented composites relative to this dataset's own history, not official or industry-standard indices. price_to_income_ratio and price_to_annual_rent_ratio use real-world units but rely on coarser-frequency, narrower-geography income and rent data.

Forecast · Bounded, mechanical baseline

The 6-month ETS forecast has no macro context and no adjustment for structural breaks like the 2020 surge or 2022 rate-hike cycle. It is a reference baseline, not a business recommendation.

HAR · Not used as structured data

HAR (Houston Association of REALTORS®) is the most locally authoritative source for Greater Houston MLS activity, but its terms of use restrict redistribution. No HAR data appears in any CSV, chart, or table in this project, including this dashboard.
